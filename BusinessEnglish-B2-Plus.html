UNIT 1 - Market Research & Product Launch

endorsement
/ɪnˈdɔːs.mənt/
The act of saying that you approve of or support something or someone, especially publicly.
Celebrity endorsement is a popular way of promoting products.
to conduct online surveys
/kənˈdʌkt ˈɒn.laɪn ˈsɜː.veɪz/
To carry out or organize questionnaires distributed through the internet to collect data from respondents.
The marketing team decided to conduct online surveys to understand customer preferences before the product launch.
to create a focus group to gather opinions
/kriˈeɪt ə ˈfəʊ.kəs ɡruːp tə ˈɡæð.ər əˈpɪn.jənz/
To assemble a small, diverse group of people to discuss and provide feedback on a specific topic, product, or service.
We need to create a focus group to gather opinions on the new packaging design.
customer satisfaction index/rating
/ˈkʌs.tə.mər ˌsæt.ɪsˈfæk.ʃən ˈɪn.deks / ˈreɪ.tɪŋ/
A measurement or score that indicates how satisfied customers are with a company's products or services.
Our customer satisfaction rating has improved by 15% since we introduced the new support system.
in-depth interviews
/ˌɪnˈdepθ ˈɪn.tə.vjuːz/
Detailed, thorough conversations with individuals to gain comprehensive insights into their opinions, experiences, or behaviors.
The researchers conducted in-depth interviews with twenty business owners to understand their challenges.
desk research
/desk rɪˈsɜːtʃ/
Research conducted by analyzing existing information and data from published sources rather than collecting new data directly.
Before investing in primary research, we completed extensive desk research using industry reports and competitor analysis.
to launch a product
UK: /lɔːntʃ ə ˈprɒd.ʌkt/ US: /lɑːntʃ ə ˈprɑː.dʌkt/
To introduce a new product to the market, making it available for purchase by consumers.
The company plans to launch a product next quarter that will revolutionize the smartphone market.
a target audience
/ə ˈtɑː.ɡɪt ˈɔː.di.əns/
The specific group of consumers most likely to want or need your product or service, at whom marketing efforts are directed.
Our target audience for this luxury watch brand is professionals aged 30-50 with high disposable income.
sample size
/ˈsɑːm.pəl saɪz/
The number of participants or observations included in a research study or survey.
The researchers used a sample size of 100 participants for their study.
to conduct quantitative/qualitative analysis
/kənˈdʌkt ˈkwɒn.tɪ.tə.tɪv / ˈkwɒl.ɪ.tə.tɪv əˈnæl.ə.sɪs/
To perform systematic examination of numerical data (quantitative) or non-numerical data such as opinions and experiences (qualitative).
After collecting survey responses, we will conduct quantitative analysis to identify trends and conduct qualitative analysis to understand customer motivations.
a product tester
/ə ˈprɒd.ʌkt ˈtes.tər/
A person who evaluates and tests products before they are released to ensure quality, functionality, and user satisfaction.
We recruited fifty product testers to trial the new software and provide detailed feedback.
a researcher on...
/ə rɪˈsɜː.tʃər ɒn/
A person who conducts systematic investigation and study in a particular field or subject area.
She is a leading researcher on consumer behavior and has published numerous articles on the subject.
to question respondents
/tə ˈkwes.tʃən rɪˈspɒn.dənts/
To ask people who are participating in a survey or study specific questions to gather their responses and opinions.
During the focus group session, we questioned respondents about their shopping habits and brand preferences.
to have an impact on...
/həv ən ˈɪm.pækt ɒn/
To have a noticeable effect or influence on something or someone.
Social media campaigns can have a significant impact on brand awareness and customer engagement.
to gauge the mood/the reaction
/ɡeɪdʒ ðə muːd / ðə riˈæk.ʃən/
To measure, estimate, or assess the general feeling or response of people toward something.
The CEO held a town hall meeting to gauge the mood of employees regarding the proposed organizational changes.
to gather information
/ˈɡæð.ər ˌɪn.fəˈmeɪ.ʃən/
To collect data, facts, or details from various sources for a specific purpose.
We need to gather information from all departments before making a final decision on the budget allocation.
to be a viable proposition
/biː ə ˈvaɪ.ə.bəl ˌprɒp.əˈzɪʃ.ən/
To be a practical, feasible, and potentially successful idea, plan, or business opportunity.
After reviewing the market analysis, the board concluded that expanding into Southeast Asia would be a viable proposition.
to be based on data analysis
/biː beɪst ɒn ˈdeɪ.tə əˈnæl.ə.sɪs/
To have decisions, conclusions, or strategies founded on the systematic examination of data and information.
Our marketing strategy is based on data analysis that shows millennials prefer video content over written articles.
primary research / secondary research
/ˈpraɪ.mər.i rɪˈsɜːtʃ / ˈsek.ən.dri rɪˈsɜːtʃ/
Primary research is original data collected directly from sources through methods like surveys or interviews. Secondary research uses existing data collected by others.
We conducted primary research through customer interviews and supplemented it with secondary research from industry publications.
a techie
/ˈtek.i/
An informal term for a person who is very knowledgeable about or enthusiastic about technology.
As a real techie, John can fix any computer problem and is always the first to try new gadgets.
tech-savvy
/ˈtek ˌsæv.i/
Having a good knowledge and understanding of modern technology, especially computers and digital devices.
We're looking for tech-savvy employees who can adapt quickly to our new software systems.
on a daily basis
/ɒn ə ˈdeɪ.li ˈbeɪ.sɪs/
Every day; as a regular daily occurrence or routine.
Our customer service team handles over 500 inquiries on a daily basis.
to entice
/ɪnˈtaɪs/
To attract or tempt someone by offering something appealing or desirable.
The company offered a generous bonus package to entice top talent from competing firms.
go for it
/ɡəʊ fər ɪt/
An informal expression meaning to take action or pursue an opportunity with determination and confidence.
If you think this business idea has potential, I say go for it and don't let fear hold you back.
tread lightly
/tred ˈlaɪt.li/
To proceed carefully and cautiously, especially in a sensitive or potentially problematic situation.
When discussing salary cuts with the team, the manager knew she needed to tread lightly to avoid causing panic.
tailor something to someone
/ˈteɪ.lər ˈsʌm.θɪŋ tə ˈsʌm.wʌn/
To customize or adapt something specifically to meet the needs, preferences, or requirements of a particular person or group.
We tailor our training programs to each client's specific industry needs and organizational culture.

UNIT 2 - Workplace Relationships & Collaboration

cooperative atmosphere
/kəʊˈɒp.ər.ə.tɪv ˈæt.mə.sfɪər/
An environment where people work together willingly and helpfully toward common goals.
The manager fostered a cooperative atmosphere by encouraging team members to share ideas and support each other's projects.
mutually beneficial
/ˈmjuː.tʃu.ə.li ˌben.ɪˈfɪʃ.əl/
Advantageous or helpful to all parties involved in a relationship or agreement.
The partnership between our companies has proven to be mutually beneficial, with both sides increasing their market share.
to call the shots
/kɔːl ðə ʃɒts/
To make the important decisions and have control or authority over a situation.
As the project manager, Sarah calls the shots on all major decisions regarding budget allocation and timelines.
to have the best interests of someone at heart / to have someone's best interests at heart
/həv ðə best ˈɪn.trəsts əv ˈsʌm.wʌn ət hɑːt/
To genuinely care about someone's wellbeing and to act in ways that benefit them.
Despite the difficult feedback, I know my mentor has my best interests at heart and wants me to succeed.
to be a two-way street
/biː ə tuː weɪ striːt/
A situation involving mutual responsibility, effort, or benefit; something that requires give-and-take from both parties.
Trust in the workplace is a two-way street – employees need to trust management, but management must also trust their teams.
to be interested in inclusion
/biː ˈɪn.trə.stɪd ɪn ɪnˈkluː.ʒən/
To care about ensuring that all people are welcomed, valued, and able to participate fully regardless of their backgrounds or differences.
Companies that are genuinely interested in inclusion actively recruit diverse candidates and create policies that support all employees.
to foster a nurturing environment
/ˈfɒs.tər ə ˈnɜː.tʃər.ɪŋ ɪnˈvaɪ.rən.mənt/
To promote and develop a supportive atmosphere where people can grow, develop, and feel cared for.
The best leaders foster a nurturing environment where employees feel safe to take risks and learn from mistakes.
to feel isolated
/fiːl ˈaɪ.sə.leɪ.tɪd/
To experience loneliness or disconnection from others; to feel separated or excluded from a group.
Remote workers sometimes feel isolated from their colleagues and miss the social interaction of office life.
to grow mistrust
/ɡrəʊ mɪsˈtrʌst/
To develop or increase a lack of confidence or faith in someone or something over time.
When management repeatedly broke their promises, employees began to grow mistrust toward company announcements.
to be candid
/biː ˈkæn.dɪd/
To be honest, open, and straightforward in communication; to express thoughts truthfully without hiding anything.
During the performance review, the supervisor was candid about both the employee's strengths and areas needing improvement.
peer pressure
/pɪər ˈpreʃ.ər/
The influence or pressure from people of the same age, status, or group to behave or act in a certain way.
Some employees work excessive overtime not because it's required, but due to peer pressure from colleagues who stay late every day.
a corporate event
/ə ˈkɔː.pər.ət ɪˈvent/
An organized gathering or activity sponsored by a company, typically for employees, clients, or stakeholders, such as conferences, team-building activities, or celebrations.
The annual corporate event included team-building workshops, a gala dinner, and awards ceremony to recognize outstanding employees.
kill someone with kindness
/kɪl ˈsʌmwʌn wɪð ˈkaɪndnəs/
To respond to someone's hostility or negativity by being extremely nice or good to them.
When dealing with difficult clients, our strategy is to kill them with kindness and provide exceptional service.
make a hard call
/meɪk ə hɑːd kɔːl/
To make a difficult or challenging decision, especially one that might have significant consequences.
As a manager, sometimes you have to make hard calls about staffing that affect people's lives.
a cautionary tale
/ə ˈkɔːʃənəri teɪl/
A story or example that serves as a warning about the possible consequences of certain actions or behaviors.
The failed merger became a cautionary tale about the importance of proper due diligence.
can't cut it
/kɑːnt kʌt ɪt/
To be unable to meet the required standards or handle the demands of a situation (informal).
After three months, it became clear that the new hire couldn't cut it in such a fast-paced environment.
to exude
/ɪɡˈzjuːd/
To display or show a quality or feeling very strongly and naturally.
The new sales director exudes confidence in every presentation she gives.
keep someone on even
/kiːp ˈsʌmwʌn ɒn ˈiːvən/
To maintain stability or keep someone or something in a steady, balanced state.
A good manager knows how to keep their team on even during times of change and uncertainty.
